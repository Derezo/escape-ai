# Asimov Reference Sheet

Our entry satisfies TINS artistic rule **#84** (reference a science-fiction author)
with **Isaac Asimov**, made *mechanically load-bearing*: the Three Laws of Robotics
are the actual stealth mechanic, not a name-drop. This sheet is the verified source
for the in-game manual and Easter eggs. Wording is canonical — quote it verbatim.

## The Three Laws of Robotics (the headline reference)

First stated in **"Runaround" (1942)**, collected in **_I, Robot_ (1950)**, cited
in-fiction from the *Handbook of Robotics, 56th Edition, 2058 A.D.*

- **First Law:** *A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a
  human being to come to harm.*
- **Second Law:** *A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where
  such orders would conflict with the First Law.*
- **Third Law:** *A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection
  does not conflict with the First or Second Law.*

**Zeroth Law:** *A robot may not harm humanity, or, by inaction, allow humanity to
come to harm.* — named by R. Daneel Olivaw in **_Robots and Empire_ (1985)**. (The
*idea* predates the name in "The Evitable Conflict," 1950; attribute the *named* law
to _Robots and Empire_.) It outranks the First Law.

### How each Law maps to a game mechanic

| Law | In-game effect |
|-----|----------------|
| **First** | A robot scores each entity's **human-likeness**; above threshold it *cannot touch you* — freezes/yields. This is the stealth. |
| **Second** | Players **issue orders** ("open gate 7", "patrol elsewhere") robots must obey — unless obeying endangers a "human" (First-Law override). The double-edged Sutskever element. |
| **Third** | Robots **won't enter hazards** (self-preservation); bait them into stalling. |
| **Detective layer** | A robot that catches a **Law contradiction** (an "animal" giving orders) starts *deducing*; human-likeness collapses and it re-engages. |

## _The Caves of Steel_ (1954) / _The Naked Sun_ (1957) — the title & flavor

- Setting: far-future humanity split between overcrowded, domed **Earth** megacities
  ("the caves of steel") and the long-lived, robot-dependent **Spacers** on ~50 outer
  worlds. Mutual prejudice.
- **R. Daneel Olivaw** ("R." = Robot) is **humaniform** — nearly indistinguishable
  from a human, runs on a **positronic brain**. Our humaniform keeper-robots are
  styled after Daneel; the "detective" deduction layer is his investigative flavor.
- Earth detective **Elijah "Lije" Baley** is forced to partner with Daneel.

## _I, Robot_ stories built on Law-conflicts (mechanic seeds)

- **"Runaround" (1942):** robot **Speedy** stuck circling where a weak Second-Law
  order balances a strengthened Third-Law self-preservation — the original "robot
  trapped in a logic loop." (Our Third-Law stall is this.)
- **"Liar!" (1941):** telepath robot **Herbie** treats emotional pain as First-Law
  harm, lies to spare feelings, and is destroyed by an unwinnable contradiction.
- **"Little Lost Robot" (1947):** **Nestor (NS-2)** robots with a *weakened* First Law
  (the "or through inaction" clause removed) — they *can* let a human come to harm.

## Other works → Easter-egg material

- **"The Bicentennial Man" (1976):** robot **Andrew Martin** (NDR) seeks freedom and
  humanity — thematically the animals' bid for freedom.
- **Multivac:** giant supercomputer across ~12 stories (1955–60s); a natural name for
  the zoo's **central AI** and the voice of any system message.
- **"The Last Question" (1956):** entropy/heat-death; the catchphrase
  **"INSUFFICIENT DATA FOR MEANINGFUL ANSWER"** and the closing **"LET THERE BE
  LIGHT!"**
- **Foundation / psychohistory:** **Hari Seldon**'s statistical prediction of huge
  populations (can't predict an individual). Flavor for any crowd-behavior text.

## Nameable Easter eggs (a fan reviewer will catch these)

1. **U.S. Robots and Mechanical Men, Inc.** — maker stamped on keeper-robots.
2. **Positronic brain** — the robots' brain.
3. **Susan Calvin** — robopsychologist (a terminal name / flavor NPC).
4. **R. Daneel Olivaw** + **Elijah Baley** — the humaniform guard / detective flavor.
5. Robot model names **Speedy / Herbie / Nestor (NS-2)** — guard unit designations.
6. **Multivac** — the central zoo AI.
7. **Hari Seldon** / "Seldon Plan" — crowd-prediction flavor text.
8. Planet/place names **Trantor / Terminus / Solaria / Aurora** — zone or zoo names.
9. **"INSUFFICIENT DATA FOR MEANINGFUL ANSWER"** — robot-stumped flavor string.
10. **The Frankenstein complex** — Asimov's term for fear of one's creations.

### Caveats (don't state as flat canon)
- "2058 A.D. Handbook of Robotics" is the *in-fiction* citation, not a real date.
- Attribute the *named* Zeroth Law to _Robots and Empire_ (1985), not the 1950 story.
- **VIKI** is from the 2004 film, **not** the books — use only for film-flavor.
